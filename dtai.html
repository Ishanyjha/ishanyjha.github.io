Overview

We present a bi-coastal, multi-institution digital twin and AI educational platform (DT/AI) for advanced microelectronics fabrication and device packaging training, developed by a collaboration spanning UC Irvine, Mercer County Community College, and Princeton University.

This Phase-2 effort extends our earlier AI-powered VR simulation framework with enhanced lithography and advanced device packaging modules, high-fidelity equipment emulations, facility-specific SOP adherence, a parallel fabricated chip-under-test with I/O verification, and tightly-coupled agentic AI engines that personalize each learner's training session.

The sections below place this paper in the context of the broader, multi-year initiative it belongs to, and preview where the work is headed next.

A growing community of practice

This paper describes Phase 2 of a multi-year, multi-institution effort to make high-fidelity digital twins of real fabrication and packaging facilities available for semiconductor workforce training.

Phase 1 began in 2024 at UC Irvine's Integrated Nanosystems Research Facility (INRF), where a team of high school and community college students demonstrated that an AI-tutored VR simulation of a cleanroom process could be designed, built, and evaluated by students themselves, using no-code authoring tools rather than a professional development team (Jha et al., 2025). That early result, and the interest it drew from other institutions, is what led directly to the bi-coastal collaboration described in this paper. Phase 3 is underway: researchers at Caltech's Kavli Nanoscience Institute are extending the same approach to a frontier etch process with the Minnich Group, discussed further below.

What ties these efforts together is not only a shared subject, but a shared way of working: a common development methodology (TC4: Capture, Create, Categorize, Customize), a shared 3D asset library, consistent evaluation instruments across cohorts, and open publication that lets each new institution build on what came before rather than starting over. Additionally, digital twin training is not a replacement of in-fab experience, but rather exists to prepare students before entering the fab, as opposed to traditional vieo and paper based educational methods. These simulations also exist to raise engagement and spark curiosity (NSF news article, 2024) in the workforce.

The journey so far

Phase 1 — UC Irvine & Pasadena City College

2024 – 2025

Working from six months of guided tours through UC Irvine's INRF cleanroom, a team of high school and community college students designed and built an AI-tutored VR simulation of the UV photolithography process using no-code authoring tools. A pilot test with 29 participants found that engagement, more than ease of use, knowledge gain, or interest change, was the strongest predictor of whether someone wanted to use more VR training in the future. The work was presented at Caltech in May 2024 and at the TechConnect World Conference, and at a rountable at the White House, the following month, drawing interest from three additional institutions

UC Irvine Photolithography workflow inside the digital twin cleanroom.

Phase 2 — Princeton University & Mercer County Community College

2025 – 2026

This paper reports the resulting bi-coastal collaboration: UC Irvine and Pasadena City College were joined by Mercer County Community College and Princeton University's Materials Institute to extend the platform to advanced device packaging and to formalize a shared development methodology. Princeton fabricated physical test chips, including one built with an intentional defect, and validated them against a custom test board, closing a real loop between the digital twin and a physical device. A second learner cohort (n = 37), using the AI-enhanced platform, independently reproduced the original finding that engagement drives future interest in the training, at a comparable effect size.

We also hosted a workshop at the TechConnect 2026 Conference in Raleigh, NC on the topic of creating these digital twins for similar processes. Extending this collaboration, we have made a community-of-practice consisting of community colleges and universities working together to develop such smulations.

Phase 3 — Caltech, Kavli Nanoscience Institute

2026 – present

The most recent extension is underway at universities including Caltech's Kavli Nanoscience Institute, where the same approach is being applied to wet-etching and advanced etch process workflows in collaboration with the Minnich Group. Unlike the largely standardized processes modeled in Phases 1 and 2, this work targets an active area of research rather than a settled teaching procedure, extending the platform from training newcomers toward onboarding researchers onto live equipment.

This phase is ongoing and has not yet been published as a paper; the demonstration below reflects work in progress.

Why this approach

Across all three phases, the people being trained and the people building the training have substantially overlapped. Students did not simply test someone else's software: they toured the facilities, authored the instructional scripts, modeled the 3D assets, and built the simulation logic themselves. That produces two outcomes from a single effort — a training tool, and a cohort of students with direct experience in VR development, instructional design, and applied AI, which are themselves among the skills the semiconductor workforce is short of.

The project has also tried to earn its claims rather than assume them. Beyond validated survey instruments, the team explicitly classified its system as an analytical digital twin operating under human oversight, with periodic rather than continuous data synchronization — a deliberately conservative claim relative to industrial real-time twins. And rather than relying on a single pilot, the core finding that engagement drives adoption was tested a second time, independently, in a different cohort using an upgraded system, and it held.

The result, across every phase, is a training tool that a handful of students and a few months of facility access can build, at a fraction of the cost of a professional studio production, and with meaningfully wider access to specific, otherwise hard-to-reach facilities.

Future directions

  • Complete the chip's lifecycleLink front-end fabrication, packaging, and advanced process modules into one traversable journey across all three facilities.
  • Adaptive, personalized tutoringExtend the agentic AI to adjust pacing and hints to individual learner history rather than a single fixed script.
  • Grow the networkPublish the TC4 methodology and shared asset library as an open toolkit, and add an industry advisory board to help prioritize future modules.
  • Close the transfer-of-training loopPartner with employers and fabs to test whether trained learners ramp faster or make fewer errors on real equipment.
